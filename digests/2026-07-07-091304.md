## OFFICIAL BLOGS

**Claude Blog: Claude Code now supports artifacts**

Claude Code just launched artifacts—a feature that turns active coding sessions into live, shareable visual pages. Instead of narrating what an agent found in a standup or Slack message, teams get a single URL that updates in real time as the session progresses: PR walkthroughs, incident timelines, system explainers, dashboards, and release checklists that fill themselves out as work gets done.

The key design decision: artifacts are built from the full context of a Claude Code session, not a manually wired data pipeline. An incident page can pull together the failing test from the codebase, an error spike from a connected monitoring tool, and the root-cause reasoning from the session itself—no setup required. Every publish creates a new version at the same URL, with full version history for restores and a gallery to browse all published artifacts.

Every artifact is private to its author by default, shareable only within the organization—admins control access with role-based scoping and retention policies.

Currently in beta for Claude Team and Enterprise orgs, available via the CLI and desktop app.
https://claude.com/blog/artifacts-in-claude-code

Claude Blog：Claude Code 现已支持 Artifacts

Claude Code 刚刚推出了 artifacts 功能——将活跃编码会话转化为实时可共享的可视化页面。团队不再需要在站会或 Slack 中描述 agent 发现了什么，而是获得一个随会话进展实时更新的 URL：PR 演练、事故时间线、系统说明文档、仪表板，以及随工作推进自动填充的发布清单。

关键设计决策：artifacts 基于 Claude Code 会话的完整上下文构建，而非手动配置的数据管道。一个事故页面可以整合代码库中的失败测试、连接的监控工具中的错误峰值，以及会话本身的根因分析——无需任何前期设置。每次发布都在同一 URL 创建新版本，保留完整版本历史以便随时恢复，还有一个画廊可以浏览所有已发布的 artifact。

每个 artifact 默认仅对作者可见，仅可在组织内部共享——管理员通过基于角色的权限和保留策略控制访问。

目前面向 Claude Team 和 Enterprise 用户开放 beta，可通过 CLI 和桌面应用访问。
https://claude.com/blog/artifacts-in-claude-code

**AI & I by Every — "Building a School Where AI Models Learn About Humanity"**

**The Takeaway:** Edwin Chen, founder and CEO of data and evals company Surge, argues that training frontier AI isn't about teaching models harder math—it's about teaching them to navigate human messiness, taste, and ambiguity. And his company, which reached $1 billion in revenue without raising outside money, sits quietly at the center of how the frontier gets built.

Edwin Chen has been working at the intersection of AI and human judgment since before the current wave. Surge provides data environments and evals for the major AI labs—doing the nuanced, expert-guided work of telling models what "good" looks like when the answer isn't clean. His framing: "We are building this school for AGI, the school where AI models come to learn about humanity—where we teach them how to run the world."

What's changed in the last year isn't just model capability—it's the nature of what they need to learn. Early benchmarks tested middle-school math (models scored around 20%); the frontier has now shifted to research-level mathematics. Surge recently launched Riemann Bench to test exactly that. When an AI model recently disproved a decades-old Erdős conjecture using novel algebraic geometry techniques, Fields Medalist Timothy Gowers first misunderstood the result—thinking the model had proved an upper bound—and was relieved when he realized it had "merely" found a counterexample, because it meant mathematicians still had a role to play for another year or two.

Edwin's observation: "You have one of the world's greatest mathematicians being relieved that AI isn't as far as he thought."

Deeper in the conversation, Edwin grapples with a harder question: if scaling laws hold and AI can do everything better than humans within five to ten years, what happens to human motivation? He invokes a Ted Chiang story—"What's Expected of Us"—about a technology that proves free will doesn't exist, where the narrator's warning sent back through time is: "You have to pretend that you have free will." His version for the AI era: we may need to consciously choose to create, learn, and prove things ourselves—not because the output is optimal, but because preserving our humanity is valuable in itself, even when AI can do it better.
