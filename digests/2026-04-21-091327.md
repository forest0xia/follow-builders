### No Priors — The Agentic Economy: How AI Agents Will Transform the Financial System with Circle Co-Founder and CEO Jeremy Allaire

**The Takeaway:** Stablecoins and blockchain infrastructure are quietly becoming the financial rails for the emerging agentic economy — and Circle's Jeremy Allaire has been building toward this moment for 13 years.

Jeremy Allaire, co-founder and CEO of Circle (the company behind USDC), makes a compelling case that the financial system needs a complete rethink for a world where AI agents conduct economic activity autonomously. USDC is backed by short-duration US Treasuries and repos — operating as full-reserve, programmable money. Unlike fractional reserve banking, it can't be lent out, which Allaire sees as a safer architecture for the financial system.

What's especially interesting is how Allaire ties stablecoins to AI agents: as more work in the economy is conducted by AI agents paying each other for services, the existing banking system simply can't handle the required transaction volumes, speed, and programmability. "We need transactions that can scale potentially into the billions or trillions," he said — something the traditional financial system was never designed for.

Circle's new blockchain project, ARC, is designed specifically for this "machine economy." Unlike Ethereum or Solana, ARC uses a known validator set of major financial infrastructure companies, delivers deterministic settlement finality in hundreds of milliseconds, uses USDC as its native token (no volatile gas fees), and ships with built-in privacy primitives from day one.

Allaire is also optimistic about tokenized real-world assets — NASDAQ and NYSE are actively moving to tokenize equities — and sees ZK rollups and zero-knowledge proofs as critical for scaling blockchain infrastructure to support billions of AI agents transacting simultaneously.

On the 10-year horizon, he predicts double-digit GDP growth in the 2030s driven by AI, but warns that the real risk is capital capturing more of that value at the expense of humans — requiring new social contracts and new organizational structures, possibly built on-chain.
