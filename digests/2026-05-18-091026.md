**Aaron Levie** (Box CEO) published two sharp takes on AI, education, and the future of technical careers. First: the job market is in a temporary mismatch — while AI makes coding abundant, every industry now needs technical talent to implement agentic systems. He's hearing this from pharma CEOs, CIOs, and leaders across nearly every sector. "The types of roles engineers should be thinking about radically expands." The demand for technical skill is there; the shape of the job is just different.

His second take is a counterweight: don't skip the fundamentals. "AI will trick you into thinking you don't need to go deep in a particular area, but that's wrong." The expert with AI will always outperform the novice. An experienced software developer building with agents outpaces someone just vibe coding. "Don't give up on going deep in your craft."

**Peter Yang** (product at Roblox, runs a 140K+ subscriber AI newsletter) shared a practical principle for building LLM evals: "Build evals based on real traces + feedback." The critical insight: as models get smarter, evals need to get harder to keep producing useful signal.

## PODCASTS

**AI & I by Every — "Claude Code Can Be Your Second Brain"**

**The Takeaway:** A home server in the basement, an Obsidian vault of notes, and Claude Code running on top — Noah Breyer has built what may be the most ambitious personal AI setup in the wild, turning his phone into a genuine thinking machine he can use anywhere.

Noah Breyer is an AI strategy consultant and co-founder of multiple startups including Percolate and Variance. He joins the AI & I podcast to walk through a setup that doesn't center on code — it centers on knowledge. He maintains a large Obsidian vault synced via Git on a home server, accessible via Claude Code from any device, including his phone. Claude Code can pull context from across his entire note archive, ask sharp clarifying questions, and keep a running log of what he's learned and what his best ideas are.

The insight that drives the whole setup: the phone used to be useless for deep work. "One of the things I find really extraordinary about it is the ability for me to work really productively on my phone," Breyer says. Writing, researching, thinking — previously the phone was a bottleneck. With Claude Code on a home server backed by a structured knowledge base, it's no longer.

He also made a bold claim for Grok voice mode: "It's way better than any of the other voice modes, and I will fight anybody who says anything different." He points to naturalness and latency — critical when thinking out loud in a car or on a walk. (He has a Tesla, where Grok is now baked in.)

The broader implication: personal AI infrastructure is no longer just for engineers. With a home server, a markdown note archive, and Claude Code as the intelligence layer, anyone can build a system that knows them deeply and augments their thinking in real time.
