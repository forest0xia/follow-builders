Claude Code can now capture session work as live, shareable visual pages called artifacts. These turn a Claude Code session's output into web pages anyone can open and explore: PR walkthroughs, dashboards you can filter and sort, incident timelines, or release checklists that fill themselves out as work gets done. Artifacts build from the full context of your session — your codebase, connected tools, and conversation — with no extra data wiring required. Version history is maintained, and a gallery lets you browse all artifacts you've created. All artifacts are private to the author by default; admins manage org-wide access through role-based scoping, retention policies, and a compliance API. Available in beta for Claude Team and Enterprise orgs via the Claude Code CLI and desktop app.

## PODCASTS

**The MAD Podcast with Matt Turck — "The Biggest Chip Ever Built — Why OpenAI Runs On It | Cerebras CEO Andrew Feldman"**
https://www.youtube.com/@DataDrivenNYC/videos

**The Takeaway:** The reason OpenAI signed a $20B+ deal with Cerebras isn't just about speed — it's that GPUs fundamentally cannot do fast inference, and Cerebras built the only architecture that can.

Andrew Feldman, cofounder and CEO of Cerebras, explains why the AI chip landscape is reorganizing around one bottleneck: decode speed. When you ask an AI a question, generating the answer token by token requires moving the model's weights — equivalent to 100 HD movies worth of data — from memory to compute, for every single word. GPUs use slow HBM memory for this step. Cerebras uses SRAM, which sits directly on the chip and is 2,500x faster. To hold enough SRAM, they built at wafer scale: 46,225 square millimeters, 58 times larger than a GPU, made from a single silicon wafer at TSMC. Feldman spent two years in 2018-2019 and $8M/month just solving the packaging problem — how to cool and power a chip the size of a dinner plate. Nobody had done it before. The chip shipped in 2020 and nobody cared, because AI was still a hobby. Then reasoning models arrived, requiring far more inference compute per answer, and suddenly Cerebras had the only architecture that scaled with demand. On CUDA's moat: "It takes eight keystrokes to move from a GPU to us in the cloud." Feldman also argues the "AI bubble" narrative misreads the market — supply is chasing demand, not the other way around — with three simultaneous shortages in HBM memory, CoWoS packaging, and 3nm factory capacity, none of which Cerebras faces due to its architectural choices. The result: the largest semiconductor IPO in history, a $20B+ deal with OpenAI for 750MW of data center capacity, and a disaggregated partnership with AWS where Trainium handles prefill and Cerebras handles decode.

**总结：** OpenAI 与 Cerebras 签下逾 200 亿美元大单，背后的核心逻辑不只是速度——而是 GPU 从根本上无法实现快速 inference，而 Cerebras 构建了唯一能做到这一点的架构。

Cerebras 联合创始人兼 CEO Andrew Feldman 解释了为什么整个 AI 芯片格局正在围绕一个瓶颈重组：decode 速度。当你向 AI 提问，逐 token 生成答案的过程需要将模型权重——相当于 100 部高清电影的数据量——从内存搬运到计算单元，每生成一个词都要重复一次。GPU 在这一步使用的是速度较慢的 HBM 内存，而 Cerebras 使用直接集成在芯片上的 SRAM，速度快 2500 倍。为了容纳足够多的 SRAM，他们把芯片做到了晶圆级：46225 平方毫米，比 GPU 大 58 倍，由台积电的整块硅晶圆制成。2018 至 2019 年间，Feldman 用了两年时间、每月花费 800 万美元，只为解决封装问题——如何冷却和供电一块餐盘大小的芯片。这是前所未有的工程挑战。芯片于 2020 年发货，但当时无人问津，因为 AI 还只是一种"爱好"。直到推理模型出现，每次回答需要消耗更多的 inference 算力，Cerebras 的架构优势才随需求爆发而彰显。关于 CUDA 的护城河，Feldman 说："从 GPU 切换到我们的云服务只需要八次键击。"他还认为"AI 泡沫"的说法误读了市场——现在是供给在追赶需求，而非相反——目前同时存在三大短缺：HBM 内存、CoWoS 封装工艺和台积电 3nm 产能，由于架构选择，Cerebras 完全不受这三者影响。最终结果：史上最大的半导体 IPO、与 OpenAI 签下逾 200 亿美元的 750MW 数据中心容量协议，以及与 AWS 的混合架构合作——Trainium 负责 prefill，Cerebras 负责 decode。
