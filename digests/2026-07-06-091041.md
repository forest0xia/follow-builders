**No Priors — Really Big Test-Time Compute in AI Changes Benchmarks, Safety and Research with OpenAI Research Scientist Noam Brown**

The Takeaway: AI model benchmarks are systematically misleading because they don't account for test-time compute — and the labs know it, but no one wants to break the bad equilibrium first.

Noam Brown, who pioneered inference-time scaling at OpenAI, argues that the standard benchmark grid — rows of models, columns of tasks, one number per cell — is fundamentally broken for today's models. It doesn't control for how much compute a model is allowed to use at inference time, and that gap matters enormously now. Modern models like GPT-5.5 can think productively for weeks on certain tasks, and their performance still hasn't plateaued. Giving a model $10 vs. $10,000 worth of inference budget can yield dramatically different results, yet benchmarks treat these as equivalent.

Brown's fix is simple: add an x-axis. Plot performance as a function of inference budget — tokens, cost, or time — rather than as a single number. Everyone agrees this is better. Nobody does it, because everyone expects the old grid. "I felt like, if I just come out and say, guys, let's all recognize we're in a bad equilibrium, and let's move to a different equilibrium — hopefully that can help."

The safety implications are uncomfortable. Responsible scaling policies and preparedness frameworks were designed in the GPT-3 era, when test-time compute scaling wasn't meaningfully possible. Now a model's capability is literally a function of how much money you put into it. Safety evaluations that don't control for inference budget may be systematically underestimating what models can do — and at what budget a model becomes dangerous.

On recursive self-improvement, Brown is measured: models are accelerating research, but they're bottlenecked by time itself. A model that needs to think for a month to solve a hard problem can't trigger an overnight intelligence explosion. "Time itself becomes a bottleneck to what we can do."

He's also bullish on untapped latent capability: OpenAI's internal model disproved the Erdős unit distance conjecture at a "dirt cheap" budget. Brown believes you could have gotten there with GPT-5.5 and a $100K scaffold — but nobody had thought to try yet. The implication: there's enormous capability overhang in models already released that the world hasn't begun to explore.

**No Priors — 测试时算力的大规模扩展如何改变 AI 的 Benchmark、安全与研究 | OpenAI 研究科学家 Noam Brown**

核心洞察：AI 模型 benchmark 存在系统性误导，因为它们根本没有控制测试时计算量（test-time compute）——各大实验室对此心知肚明，却没有人愿意第一个打破这个糟糕的均衡。

OpenAI 推理时扩展的先驱 Noam Brown 指出，当前比较 AI 模型的主流方式存在根本缺陷。那张熟悉的 benchmark 表格——模型一行、任务一列、每格一个数字——没有控制模型在推理阶段被允许使用多少算力，而这个缺口在今天至关重要。GPT-5.5 这样的现代模型可以在某些任务上"思考"数周，且性能仍未达到平台期。给模型 $10 还是 $10,000 的推理预算，结果可能天壤之别，但 benchmark 将两者等同对待。

Brown 提出的解决方案很简单：加一条 x 轴。用推理预算（token 数、成本或时间）作为横轴，呈现性能曲线，而不是单一数字。人人都同意这样更好。但没有人这么做，因为大家都期待看到旧格式。"我想，如果我直接站出来说：好，大家承认我们陷入了一个糟糕的均衡，让我们一起迈向新的均衡——也许能推动改变。"

安全层面的含义令人不安。负责任扩展政策和预备框架是在 GPT-3 时代设计的，那时测试时算力扩展根本无从实现。而现在，模型能力实际上是投入算力的函数。不控制推理预算的安全评估，可能系统性地低估了模型的真实能力——以及在什么预算规模下模型会变得危险。

谈到递归自我改进，Brown 态度审慎：模型正在加速研究进程，但受到时间本身的制约。一个需要思考一个月才能解决难题的模型，不可能引发一夜之间的智能爆炸。"时间本身成了我们能做什么事的瓶颈。"

他同时对潜在能力保持乐观：OpenAI 的内部模型以"极低成本"证伪了 Erdős 单位距离猜想。Brown 认为用 GPT-5.5 加上 $10 万的算力脚手架本可以更早做到——只是没有人想到要去尝试。这意味着：已发布模型中存在巨大的能力储备，世界还远未开始探索。
