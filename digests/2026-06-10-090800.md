**AI & I by Every — "We Automated Everything With AI and Tripled Our Headcount"**

**The Takeaway:** AI doesn't eliminate demand for human expertise — it floods every workplace with good-enough work that requires more experts to finish, not fewer.

Every CEO Dan Shipper built one of the most AI-native media companies in existence: if you throw a stick in their Slack, you're as likely to hit an agent as a human. Since the GPT-3 era, they've grown from 4 to 30 people and are still hiring. Rather than see this as a paradox, Shipper has a clean explanation.

AI, he argues, makes "yesterday's expert competence cheap." It trains on all previous outputs — code, writing, design, decisions — and makes that available to everyone for near-zero cost. The immediate result: everyone starts producing things that are close to expert quality but not quite right for the specific situation. That floods the zone with work that looks good, devalues generic output, and dramatically increases demand for real experts who can take the AI-assisted near-miss and get it across the finish line.

The counter-intuitive insight: "The further away an agent gets from a human, the less valuable it is." The human-agent pairing is what makes the work actually work. At Every, they've seen this repeatedly — an engineer like Kieran builds an entire inbox end-to-end in a month or two, which would have been "completely impossible" before. But that only happened because an expert was in the loop guiding it.

On the question of AGI displacing all work, Shipper offers a structural argument: "We are building agents to do things that we want them to do. No matter how powerful they get, all of the economic and psychological and technological forces are pushing the progress of AI toward a place where... it's looking back at you to decide what you want to do, what is valuable." Even after AGI, an agent will still be built to serve human direction — because no one would build one that didn't.

The ClickUp CEO who fired 20%+ of his workforce and bragged about it? Shipper's read: the company probably isn't doing that well, the bragging is a tell, and poor AI implementation that leads to rehiring two months later is exactly what happens when leaders who haven't actually used agents make sweeping decisions.

"If you just ride the models, you're going to be fine. If you care about leading a really ambitious life, I truly think that this is going to make that more possible for more people."
https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLuMcoKK9mKgHtW_o9h5sGO2vXrffKHwJL

**结论：** AI 不会消灭对人类专业能力的需求，它只会让每个工作场所充斥着"差不多够用"的工作成果，而这反而需要更多专家来收尾，而不是更少。

Every CEO Dan Shipper 打造了当今最 AI-native 的媒体公司之一：他们 Slack 里的 agent 和人类数量几乎相当。自 GPT-3 时代以来，公司从 4 人扩张到 30 人，还在继续招聘。Shipper 对这个看似矛盾的现象有一套清晰的解释。

他认为，AI 让"昨天的专家能力变得廉价"。它训练于所有过去的产出——代码、写作、设计、决策——并以接近零成本将这些能力提供给所有人。直接结果是：每个人都开始产出接近专家水准但又不完全适配当前情境的内容。这让工作场所充斥着看起来不错却差一口气的成果，使通用产出贬值，同时大幅提升了对真正专家的需求——他们需要介入，把 AI 辅助的"接近正确"推过终点线。

反直觉的洞见："agent 离人越远，价值越低。"人机协作才是让工作真正奏效的关键。Every 亲身验证了这一点：工程师 Kieran 用一两个月独立构建了整个收件箱产品，这在以前"完全不可能"。但这只因为有专家全程参与引导。

关于 AGI 是否会取代所有工作，Shipper 给出了一个结构性论点："我们构建 agent 是为了做我们想让它做的事。无论它变得多强大，所有经济、心理和技术力量都在推动 AI 朝着一个方向发展：它始终在回望你，等你来决定什么重要、什么有价值。"即使在 AGI 之后，agent 仍然会被构建为服务于人类方向——因为没人会建造一个不这样做的 agent。

对于那位裁员 20%+ 员工还大肆宣扬的 ClickUp CEO？Shipper 的判断：公司大概率经营不善，炫耀本身是个信号，而草率导入 AI 后两个月再重新招人的情况，正是那些从未真正用过 agent 的领导者做出大规模决策时的典型结局。

"如果你只是随着模型发展顺势而为，你会没问题。如果你真正在乎过上一种有野心的生活，我真心认为这会让更多人实现这一点。"
