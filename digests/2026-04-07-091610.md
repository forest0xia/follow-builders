**Peter Yang** — Product at Roblox, AI educator with 140K+ newsletter readers

In a wide-ranging conversation with a16z GP Anish Acharya, Peter Yang laid out five shifts he sees reshaping work in an AI-agent-first world. First: coding is eating all knowledge work — Yang says he now gets "the first 80% done through AI coding agents before doing manual polish for the last 20%." Second: small, tight teams will outperform bloated orgs — he and Anish both recalled sitting through three-hour OKR meetings and concluded that 2–3 person product teams with a swarm of agents will displace them. Third: apps built to complete tasks will shrink, while apps that entertain will survive. Fourth: personal agents will know you better than you expect — his own agent told him he keeps talking about career and business but his kids are 7 and 4 and growing up fast. Fifth: human ambition has no ceiling. On a separate note, Yang pushed back on measuring developer productivity by token usage: "I don't know — measuring productivity by token usage sounds almost as dumb as measuring by lines of code written."

**Aaron Levie** — CEO of Box

Levie pushes back on the idea that AI agents are eliminating work — he argues they're just moving it up a layer of abstraction. With agents doing the execution, the new work becomes: figuring out what to tell the agent, giving it proper instructions, getting it the right context, catching it when it goes off track, and reviewing the output. "You are now the editor. A manager. A producer." He's direct: taste matters, understanding the craft matters, skills matter. AI agents are automating the annoying parts and leaving the more enjoyable ones. The work is still there — just different.

**Latent Space — Mistral: Voxtral TTS, Forge, Leanstral, & what's next for Mistral 4 — w/ Pavan Kumar Reddy & Guillaume Lample**

**The Takeaway:** Mistral just launched Voxtral TTS, a 3B speech-generation model that challenges the assumption that you need a giant general model to do audio well — and the architecture behind it is genuinely novel.

Guillaume Lample, Mistral's Chief Scientist, and Pavan Kumar Reddy, who leads audio research after stints at Google Gemini and Google Assistant, walked through the full Mistral audio stack. Voxtral TTS is a 3-billion-parameter model built on top of the Ministral backbone, supporting nine languages. The design philosophy is deliberate: instead of a massive multimodal model that does everything adequately, Mistral is building small, specialized models optimized for specific tasks. The TTS model costs a fraction of its competitors.

The novel architecture uses what Pavan calls an "autoregressive flow matching" approach. In text generation, predicting the next token is relatively deterministic. But the same word spoken out loud can be pronounced in dozens of different ways — with different intonation, rhythm, and emphasis. Standard discrete-token approaches average across those possibilities and produce blurred, unnatural speech. Flow matching, borrowed from the image generation world, models that distribution explicitly: instead of predicting a single token, it estimates a velocity field from noise to the target audio latent, arriving at a sharp, natural-sounding output. "You have to pick one [inflection] — the intuition I have is there are different clusters, each corresponding to some specific way you would inflect, pronounce that thing."

The real-time streaming requirement shaped everything. Voice agents can't wait for a full audio clip to be generated — they need low-latency, chunk-by-chunk output. Flow matching lets Mistral do inference in as few as 4 or 8 steps (versus K steps per token with a depth transformer), and the framework in principle supports single-step inference for even faster output.

Mistral's roadmap goes from transcription to TTS to real-time conversational audio to, eventually, full-duplex models that can listen and speak simultaneously. Guillaume is candid that even the best voice AI today doesn't yet feel like talking to a person — particularly in non-English languages, where people instinctively slow down and over-articulate. Closing that gap is the mission.

**The Takeaway:** Mistral 刚刚发布了 Voxtral TTS，一个30亿参数的语音生成模型，挑战了"做好音频必须要有超大通用模型"的假设，其背后的架构是真正的创新。

Mistral 首席科学家 Guillaume Lample 和音频研究负责人 Pavan Kumar Reddy（曾任职于 Google Gemini 和 Google Assistant）详细介绍了 Mistral 整个音频技术栈。Voxtral TTS 基于 Ministral 底座构建，30亿参数，支持九种语言。设计哲学明确：不追求一个什么都能做、但每样都平庸的庞大多模态模型，而是为特定任务构建小而精的专用模型。该 TTS 模型的成本仅为竞争对手的一小部分。

其核心创新在于 Pavan 称之为"自回归 flow matching"的架构。文本生成中，预测下一个 token 相对确定。但同一个词大声说出来，可能有数十种不同的语调、节奏和重音。传统离散 token 方法对这些可能性做平均，产出模糊、不自然的语音。Flow matching 借鉴图像生成领域，显式地对这种分布建模：不是预测单一 token，而是估计从噪声到目标音频 latent 的速度场，最终输出清晰、自然的音频。"你必须选一个发音方式——我的直觉是存在不同的簇，每个簇对应一种特定的语调或发音方式。"

实时流式传输的需求主导了整个架构选择。语音 agent 无法等待完整音频生成，需要低延迟、逐块输出。Flow matching 让 Mistral 仅用4到8步即可完成推理（而 depth transformer 每个 token 需要 K 步），该框架原则上还支持单步推理以进一步提速。

Mistral 的路线图从转录到 TTS，再到实时对话音频，最终走向全双工模型——能够同时听和说。Guillaume 坦言，即便是现有最好的语音 AI，与真人对话的感觉依然差距明显，在非英语语言中尤为突出，人们本能地放慢语速、字正腔圆。弥合这一差距，正是 Mistral 音频团队的使命。
